We Killed Seasons, 2026
Multichannel video, sound stereo
26:40 min.











We Killed Seasons (2026) follows five people living in a world where the seasons have disappeared. Unfolding slowly and bathed in sunlight, this film installation explores the eradication of natural cycles as fundamental temporal landmarks, disrupting the human and animal rhythms. The place seem familiar, albeit being profoundly transformed.
None of them meet, except for two who discuss, among other things, the changing representation of the sun. One tries to make sense of a world where time has come undone, while another wanders aimlessly. A fifth breaks into a solitary, almost hysterical dance, revealing a profound sene of longing.

By Lorenza Kaib

In the film, two people engage in a loose conversation about what things used to be like, how things came to this point, and what the situation is now. There is also another narrative voice that attempts to frame everything. In calm scenes, the protagonists are seen interacting with their surroundings. A dead wasp, a pile of leaves, a whale: all appearing in places and situations that feel unnatural, removed from their original context. Five protagonists appear in total, each of them alone. Only the two people who are speaking to one another are connected. Otherwise, there are no encounters, reinforcing the impression of increasing isolation.

When everything is synchronized, it feels like everything is working. Something arrhythmic, and all is out of balance (excerpt from the film).


The sun, the changing meanings it has acquired throughout history, and the continued recognition of its power are among the subjects discussed by the film's protagonists. From there, they speak about the weather, rhythms, and myths. According to Greek mythology, the origin of the seasons lies in an agreement between the gods. This agreement was made after Hades abducted Persephone, the daughter of Demeter. It was decided that Persephone would spend part of the year with her mother Demeter and the other part with Hades in the underworld. With her coming and going, life on Earth also changes: plants grow and wither, and the length of the day increases and decreases.


Arseneau explores the loss of temporal points of reference. The film moves between longing, loneliness, and the subtle breakdown of the psyche. The disappearance of the seasons becomes a metaphor for the elimination of essential human rhythms—a loss of natural cycles that makes the uninterrupted exploitation of resources and labour, around the clock, possible.


- Lorenza Kaib, excerpt [my translation]
